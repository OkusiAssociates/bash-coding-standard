Part XI — Process Management

Bash sits at the intersection of the shell language and the Unix process model. This Part documents how Bash creates, tracks, signals, and manages processes — its own and its children.

Chapters

  1. 11.1 The Bash process tree at runtime — A running Bash script has a process tree shape determined by its constructs.
  2. 11.2 PIDs: $$, $BASHPID, $PPID — Three variables, three different meanings.
  3. 11.3 Subshell origins — Constructs that fork a subshell.
  4. 11.4 BASH_SUBSHELL depth tracking — Bash maintains a counter of subshell depth.
  5. 11.5 Foreground vs background — Bash distinguishes foreground commands (the shell waits for them) from background (started with &).
  6. 11.6 Process groups and sessions — The kernel groups processes into process groups and sessions for signal delivery and terminal control.
  7. 11.7 The job table — When job control is on, bash maintains a table of jobs.
  8. 11.8 Job specifications — Jobs can be referenced by several syntaxes.
  9. 11.9 Job-control builtins — Manipulate the job table.
  10. 11.10 kill and signal delivery — The kill builtin sends signals.
  11. 11.11 nohup and setsid — Decoupling from the controlling terminal.
  12. 11.12 Detaching from the terminal — Comprehensive detachment for daemons.
  13. 11.13 Environment inheritance — Children inherit the environment at fork+exec.
